The current blog is from my ongoing internship at a hospital after four and half years of Medicinae Baccalaureus Baccalaureus Chirurgiae commonly known as MBBS. It’s about something I experienced at the midnight following the last day of my Obstetrics and Gynaecology posting. Something I couldn’t sleep to.

Just like Emergency, Labor Room also works around the clock because hey! I live in India where every 2 seconds a baby is born and even lockdown cannot do anything about it. I had my Labor Room shift of 12 hours from 9 PM to 9 AM the next morning.

At around 1:00 AM, a patient entered the labor room at POG = 37 weeks. For those who aren’t familiar with this term, POG or Period of Gestation is the duration of pregnancy from the first day of the Last Menstrual Period till the current visit, which was 37 weeks or eight and a half months. The history was taken by a 2nd year Junior Resident (JR) and she discussed the case with the Senior Resident. Being an intern, I was taking blood sample of some other patient and was unaware about the chief complaints of the newcomer. After the 2nd year JR was done examining her, I had to secure an intravenous (i.v.) line to give her medications by applying cannula to her hand. For that, she sat on the stool in front of me. As I began to apply the cannula, she was constantly moving and was not keeping her hand still. This could have led to wrong placement of the cannula and ultimately, swelling of her forearm by the medications provided. So I scolded her for not cooperating. It was then, when I looked at her face for the first time. It was all covered with sweat, glowing, with eyes closed and few creases on her glabella. Clearly, she was in pain. That moment, I felt that I had been too mean in scolding her. Patient care is the most important thing and I should have cooperated instead. Thereafter I didn’t bother her and somehow, managed to apply the cannula.

After some time I was asked to take her blood pressure, which I took on the right arm. I used Digital sphygnomanometer to assess the BP and side by side checked the BP by palpatory method. Now palpatory method gives us a rough idea about the range that systolic BP might fall in. Digital BP apparatus gave a reading of systolic in 60s and diastolic in 50s, which is a hypotensive state. I only took one reading and did not recheck because by palpatory method also systolic BP was definitely way below 90 mm of Hg.

In response to this, she was rushed 500ml of Normal Saline solution by the co-intern in order to get her BP back to normal and he ran some necessary investigations such as Hemoglobin and Ultrasound after the patient was stabilized.

At around 4 AM, I monitored the fetal heart rate of all the patients who were in stage 1 of labor. Stage 1 of labor prepares the birth canal to dilate to its maximum limit so that stage 2 (delivery) can occur. So, monitoring fetal heart rate during stage 1 actually helps to know the well-being of the fetus. If there is any fetal distress, it will be reflected in the heart rate in the form of decreasing rate known as Bradycardia or increasing rate known as Tachycardia. Accordingly, we plan for C-section. That patient was the last one to be monitored by me. I started localising the fetal heart using my stethoscope. There was something unusual about her abdomen. It had blue-green marks over it, which I thought to be engorged veins because of the pregnancy itself. I examined her for approximately 2 minutes and I couldn’t listen to the beats of the fetal heart. I failed to localise it and informed the 2nd year JR, when she told me that she had an INTRAUTERINE DEAD FETUS, the duration of which was unknown to us.

For eight and a half months, you go through a list of emotions, discomfort, nausea, vomiting, mood swings, morning sickness and what not, only to know that it was all in vain. Thinking about this I resumed my work and kept on writing the delivery notes.

Then at 7:15 AM, she was given an antihypertensive -Labetalol i.v. which is given when a patient has hypertensive urgency. I was interrogating some other patient, so I asked the co-intern about her status and he told me that her BP elevated to systolic 213 mm of Hg.

I was shocked.

She came to us in a hypotensive state, and now she has a high BP within a few hours. How is that possible? And with such a high BP, pregnant females are more prone to seizures. The whole staff was talking about it and I think that’s what happened next.

At around 7:30 AM, I went to see the patient myself and saw that a 2nd year JR was holding her head to one side, as she was aspirating the food content from her mouth and nose. It appeared to me that the patient was throwing a seizure. It was scary for me because I hadn’t seen people throwing fits live in front of me, and she was pregnant too. In just a minute, the JR started giving CPR to the patient. I was so confused, that if it was a seizure, this is not how we manage it. CPR is given when there are no vital signs recordable in a patient.

In a snap of fingers, it struck me whether I had recorded the BP incorrectly. Was it correct to rush the NS? Or was Ringer Lactate the better solution to be given? She came to us wincing in pain just like any other woman who is in labor. Then how could have I differentiated between the pain she was having and the pain other women were going through, so that I could pay more attention towards her? Her big abdomen was moving with the CPR.

Just imagine for a second a CPR given to a 9 months pregnant.

My mind was getting numb to see what you just imagined, and I ran to call the anaesthesia people to do the needful. By the time I came back after informing the anaesthesiologist, 3rd year JR had also come who was now giving the CPR. Adrenaline 1 ampule was given i.v. by that time. CPR was continued by anaesthesiologist, with administration of second dose of Adrenaline. They secured the airway by intubating and attaching the Artificial Manual Breathing Unit or ambu bag. Hope established when the Carotid pulsations were felt by them. Other patients were spectating each and every move. I can’t imagine how devastating it must have been for them to go through the labor pain and the scene, altogether. Some might have empathised and might have been horrified by the very sight of it. For 5-10 minutes the CPR was continued but ultimately, the patient collapsed.

I am running short of words to portray the scene but it was real and it was overwhelming for me. Never in my Final year and Internship had I seen a death in the Labour Room till that day. This was a rare thing to happen in this hospital. Chaperones weren’t informed yet and no one was ready for this herculean task. Although the high risk was explained to them, but everybody was so scared. It was Senior Resident who broke the news to her chaperones.

Witnessing mortality is a part of my field, but I always keep very high hopes from CPR and that’s what I had for this patient too. I heard the 2nd year JR considering her for a condition called Dissaminated Intravascular Coagulopathy or DIC by sending D-dimer test at the time of examination when she was in hypotension. But the sudden rise in BP couldn’t be explained by DIC.

What happened then?

In the midnight following that day, I was lying in bed when a thought loop started building in my mind. It was an Infinite Loop of a single thought –Did I take the BP incorrectly? Even though I knew that I had not made a mistake, the mind was doing its part, disdaining truth, playing tricks incessantly, making me feel guilty and upsetting me. Turning sides on my bed, I was completely occupied by self doubt. Well, this was not something new but this time it was something very intense. Putting an end to this loop, I decided to go out for stargazing. I had watched the Fullmetal Alchemist recently then, and from there I remembered an Ishbal Chief who quoted these lines in one of the episodes –Even in the darkest of suffering a man can sleep. But for the man who inflicts suffering, his mind cannot rest. That night I realised how important it was for me to take note and be responsible for my each and every action, be it taking pulse or BP. I learnt that life is all about maintaining pulse, BP and respiratory rate. The whole CPR thing revolves around the three. And that’s why they are called the vitals and I would never hesitate to monitor them a hundred times!
